        7. HOW CAN YOU LOCATE THE POSITION OF YOUR BABY INSIDE?

        The LEOPOLD'S MANUEVER is the proper technique to determine the position of the fetus inside you, there are only 4 basic techniques that you need to follow.

      1. Fundal Grip- This is the very first step, you need to palpate the upper abdomen to determine the location of the fetal head and buttocks.
      2. Lateral Grip- Next is to palpate the sides of the abdomen to feel for the spine and limbs, this could also help you to locate the fetal heart tone
      3. Lower Pole- This determines the presenting part of the fetus by palpating the lower abdomen. It helps to determine if the head or the bottom of the fetus is positioned there.
        4. Pelvic Grip- This is the final step that involves the palpating of the pelvis to assess the engagement of the fetal presenting part and estimate its descent into the birth canal

    Breastfeeding positions

  • Cradle Position
    •

    This is one of the most common breastfeeding positions. You sit upright with your baby's head resting in the crook of your arm on the same side as the breast you are nursing from. Your baby's body is facing yours, and their mouth is aligned with your nipple.

  • Football hold
    •

    In this position, you tuck your baby under your arm like a football, with their legs pointing towards your back. Support their head with your hand and bring them to your breast from the side. This position is particularly helpful for mothers who had a cesarean birth or for babies who have difficulty latching.

  • Side-Lying Position
    •

    This position is ideal for breastfeeding while lying down, especially during nighttime feedings. Lie on your side with your baby facing you. Bring your baby close to your breast, and they can latch on while lying down. Use pillows to support your head, neck, and back for added comfort.

  • Cross-Cradle-Hold
    •

    In this position, you hold your baby with the opposite arm from the breast you are nursing from. Use your hand to support your baby's neck and head, bringin You sent bringing them close to your breast. This position can provide better control and support for newborns or babies who need assistance with latching.

  • Laid-Back or Biological Nurturing Position
    •

    This position involves reclining in a semi-reclined or fully reclined position, with your baby lying on top of your body. You can support your baby's head and neck, and they can find their way to your breast and latch on their own.

    Postpartum Recovery

    After giving birth, focus on:
    • Rest and recovery
    • Healthy eating habits
    • Gentle exercise, as advised by healthcare providers
    • Emotional well-being and seeking support if needed
    • Follow-up appointments with healthcare providers
    • Practice good hygiene, including regular showering and changing of sanitary pads
    • Engage in activities that promote relaxation and stress reduction.

      • You should always remember to bond with your baby
    • Engage skin-to-skin contact and cuddling
    • Breastfeed or bottle-feed your baby as recomended by your health provider
    • Talk,sing, and read to your baby to promote bonding and language development

        • Remember, postpartum recovery is a gradual process,and it's important to be patient with yourself. Listen to your body, seek help when needed, and celebrate small milestones along the way
